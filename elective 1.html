Output & Utilization

This section summarizes the expected electrical output and practical ways the village can utilize that energy to maximize social and economic benefit.

Typical Outputs (Illustrative)

  • Small Barrage/Lagoon: ≈ 5,000 kWh/day → ~209 kW average power.
  • Tidal-Stream Farm: ≈ 16.4 kW per 3.6 m turbine at 2 m/s → 10 turbines ≈ 164 kW rated.

Energy Utilization Strategy

  1. Critical Services: water supply, clinic refrigeration, street lighting, communication systems.
  2. Local Livelihood: cold storage, ice-making, agro-processing, and small-scale industry.
  3. Household Use: lighting, fans, phone charging, and small appliances.
  4. Economic Growth: battery charging stations and small enterprises during high generation periods.

Storage & Load Shifting

Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) can smooth generation. Example: evening peak 150 kW × 6 h → battery ≈ 900 kWh.

1) Key Site Selection Checks

  1. Tidal Range: Measure spring & neap ranges (H).
  2. Bathymetry: Seabed type affects foundation cost.
  3. Flow Velocities: Key for turbine yield estimates.
  4. Coastal Form: Narrow inlet suits barrage; open coast suits stream turbines.
  5. Environmental/Social Constraints: Include mangroves, fisheries, and community consultation.

2) Option A — Small Tidal Barrage / Lagoon

Water is stored in a basin at high tide and released through turbines during ebb tide to generate power.

Components

  • Concrete barrage or embankment
  • Sluice gates
  • Kaplan or bulb turbines
  • Switchyard & step-up transformer

Energy Example

E = 0.5 × ρ × g × A × H² × η

Example result: ≈ 2,514 kWh per tidal cycle → ≈ 5,000 kWh/day → ~209 kW average.

3) Option B — Tidal-Stream Turbines

In-stream turbines convert kinetic energy of flowing tides directly into electricity.

Formula

P = 0.5 × ρ × A × Cp × V³

Example: one 3.6 m turbine → 16.4 kW at 2 m/s; 10 turbines → ~164 kW total.

4) Electrical Architecture

  • Generation: 400–690 V → Step-up to 11 kV or 33 kV.
  • Distribution: Microgrid or grid-tied depending on village scale.
  • Storage: BESS for night supply and stability.
  • Load Management: Efficient appliances and peak scheduling.

5) Environmental & Technical Design Notes

  • Use corrosion-resistant materials and fish-friendly blades.
  • Implement SCADA and remote monitoring systems.
  • Conduct environmental impact assessment (EIA) early.

6) Operation & Maintenance

  • Regular inspection and cleaning every 3–6 months.
  • Train local technicians and ensure spare part availability.
  • Use predictive maintenance tools where feasible.

7) Cost & Timeline (Overview)

Barrage: Higher civil costs, longer construction.

Stream Turbines: Modular, faster deployment, scalable to demand.

8) Project Planning Steps

  1. Pre-feasibility: Collect tidal data and community input.
  2. Feasibility Study & EIA.
  3. Engineering Design & Procurement.
  4. Construction & Commissioning.
  5. Training & Operations Start.
