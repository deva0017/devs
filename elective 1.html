Brief Explanation

Tidal power plants harness energy from the rise and fall of ocean tides, which are driven by the moon and sun. Water is stored in a tidal basin during high tide and released through turbines when the tide recedes. The turbines drive generators to produce electricity. A power conditioning system ensures the voltage and frequency are stable before sending it through distribution lines to the village. Tidal energy is renewable, predictable, and environmentally friendly. For coastal villages, it provides reliable electricity for homes, fisheries, irrigation, and community services, making it a sustainable energy solution.

Source of Energy for a Tidal Power Plant

The primary source of energy in a tidal power plant is the gravitational pull of the moon and sun on the Earth, which causes the rise and fall of ocean tides. The movement of water during high and low tides creates a predictable flow of kinetic and potential energy. In a coastal village setup, this tidal energy is captured by constructing a tidal basin or estuary. During high tide, water is stored in the basin, creating a difference in water levels (head) between the sea and basin. When the tide recedes, water flows through turbines, converting the stored potential energy into mechanical energy. This natural, renewable source is predictable, reliable, and environmentally friendly, making it ideal for providing decentralized electricity to small coastal communities.

Output & Utilization

This section summarises the expected electrical output from the two candidate systems and practical ways the village can utilize that energy to maximize social and economic benefit.

Typical outputs (illustrative)

  • Small barrage / lagoon (example): ~2,500 kWh per tidal cycle or ~5,000 kWh/day → ~209 kW average power.
  • Tidal-stream farm (example): ~16.4 kW per 3.6 m-diameter turbine at 2.0 m/s. Ten turbines ≈ 164 kW rated (fluctuating with tides).

How to utilize the energy — prioritized strategy

  1. Critical services: water supply pumps, community clinic refrigeration, street lighting and communications (radio/Internet gateway).
  2. Local livelihood: fish/freezer cold storage, ice making, rice milling, small-scale agro-processing — these increase local income and create steady daytime loads that match tidal generation.
  3. Household electricity: lights, phones, fans, small appliances. Promote LED and efficient appliances to stretch supply.
  4. Economic smoothing: community battery charging stations and managed EV/e-bike charging during generation peaks; enable micro-enterprises (carpentry, welding) that run on daytime generation.

Storage & load-shifting

Use a BESS sized to cover evening peaks or a target autonomy. Example: if evening peak is 150 kW for 6 hours, battery needed ≈ 900 kWh (plus losses). Batteries also improve power quality and islanding.

1) Key site selection checks (do these first)

  1. Tidal range (meters): measure typical spring & neap ranges (H).
  2. Bathymetry & seabed: rocky / sandy / soft — affects foundation choices & cost.
  3. Flow velocities (m/s): channel velocities at spring tide — essential for turbine yield estimates.
  4. Coastal morphology: narrow inlet → good for barrage; wide open coast → stream turbines.
  5. Environmental & social constraints: mangroves, fisheries, navigation, sacred sites — early consultation required.

2) Option A — Small tidal barrage / lagoon (how it works)

Principle: create a basin behind a barrier; let tide fill and release through turbines to generate power on ebb and/or flood.

Main components

  • Concrete/rockfill barrage or embankment across inlet.
  • Sluice gates to control filling/emptying.
  • Bulb or Kaplan turbines with powerhouse (low head, high flow).
  • Intake and tailrace channels.
  • Switchyard, step-up transformer, distribution lines or battery+inverter if off-grid.
  • Control/SCADA and trash screens.

Layout

Barrage across inlet with powerhouse near low point; switchyard onshore; access road and maintenance area.

Example energy estimate (worked numeric example)

Assumptions (example village site):

  • Basin area, A = 500,000 m² (0.5 km²).
  • Tidal range, H = 3.0 m.
  • Seawater density, ρ = 1025 kg/m³.
  • Gravity, g = 9.81 m/s².
  • Round-trip efficiency, η = 0.40 (40%).

Potential energy per tidal cycle (simplified barrage formula):

E = 0.5 × ρ × g × A × H² × η

Step-by-step arithmetic (digit by digit):

  1. H² = 3.0 × 3.0 = 9.0 (m²).
  2. ρ × g = 1025 × 9.81 = 10061.25.
  3. Compute full: 0.5 × 1025 × 9.81 × 500,000 × 9.0 × 0.40 = 9,049,725,000 J per tidal cycle.
  4. Convert to kWh: 9,049,725,000 J ÷ 3,600,000 = 2,513.8125 kWh per cycle.
  5. Two usable cycles/day ≈ ~5,027.625 kWh/day → average ~209.5 kW.

Interpretation: at ~209 kW average this barrage could supply ~1,675 households if average household consumption is 3 kWh/day, or community loads such as cold storage, pumps and small industry.

3) Option B — Tidal-stream (in-stream) turbines (how it works)

Principle: place horizontal- or vertical-axis turbines in tidal currents; power scales with the cube of water speed.

Main components

  • Multiple in-stream turbines mounted on fixed foundations, gravity anchors, or floating moorings.
  • Subsea cable to shore and onshore switchgear + transformer.
  • Service vessel mooring, control systems, maintenance access.

Power of a single turbine (example)

P = 0.5 × ρ × A × Cp × V³

Assumptions: ρ = 1025 kg/m³, Cp = 0.40, V = 2.0 m/s, swept area A = 10 m².

Compute:

  1. V³ = 2.0³ = 8.0.
  2. 0.5 × ρ = 512.5.
  3. 512.5 × A = 5,125; × Cp (0.40) = 2,050.
  4. 2,050 × V³ = 16,400 W → 16.4 kW per turbine (at that speed).

If you install 10 turbines → ≈ 164 kW total (theoretical); daily energy ≈ 3,936 kWh/day (subject to tidal variation and capacity factor).

Interpretation: with 10–15 modular turbines you can scale to meet a village demand of a few hundred kW peak. Turbines are modular and lower impact than big civil works.

4) Electrical architecture & utilization for a coastal village

  • Power collection: subsea collection bus (stream farm) or powerhouse collector (barrage) → onshore switchyard.
  • Transformer: generate at 400–690 V, transform to 11 kV or 33 kV for local distribution (or as site requires).
  • Distribution options:
    • Islanded microgrid: generator(s) → distribution + BESS + inverter for smoothing and night supply.
    • Grid-tied: feed into regional grid with protection and power-quality equipment.
  • Storage sizing example: evening peak 150 kW for 6 hours → battery ≈ 900 kWh (add inverter/roundtrip losses).

Load management & utilization

Prioritize water pumps, clinic refrigeration, and livelihood appliances. Encourage efficient appliances and schedule heavy loads to tidal generation windows.

5) Civil, mechanical & electrical design notes (practical)

  • Foundations: monopiles or gravity foundations depending on depth & seabed.
  • Turbine selection: low-RPM, robust to debris and biofouling; proven drivetrain components.
  • Corrosion protection: cathodic protection and marine-grade materials.
  • Marine access: dredged access channel and offshore moorings for maintenance vessel.
  • Control/protection: SCADA, remote monitoring, emergency stop.
  • Fish/wildlife mitigation: fish-friendly blades, screening, seasonal operation adjustments.

Environmental and social considerations

Barrage: can alter estuary ecology, sediment transport and fisheries — requires EIA and social consultation.

Stream turbines: lower footprint but can affect fisheries and navigation; array planning and stakeholder engagement required.

6) Operation & maintenance (O&M)

  • Routine marine inspections every 3–6 months.
  • Remove biofouling, check bearings, perform electrical diagnostics.
  • Keep spare parts stock and train a local O&M team with remote expert support.

7) Cost & timeline (broad guidance)

Barrage: high capital (USD millions) — civil works dominate; consider only if regional use or combined benefits justify.

Tidal-stream farm: modular costs historically ~USD 4,000–8,000/kW but site-dependent; a 150–300 kW village system may cost hundreds of thousands to a few million USD.

8) Decision checklist & quick project plan

  1. If tidal range ≥ 3 m and enclosed bay with low sensitivity → consider small barrage/lagoon.
  2. If range < 3 m or you want modular lower-impact deployment → tidal-stream turbines recommended.
  3. Faster deployment & lower upfront civil works → tidal-stream with a few turbines + small BESS + microgrid.

Project phases

  1. Pre-feasibility: tidal data (1 year preferable), bathymetry, stakeholder mapping.
  2. Feasibility & EIA: yield estimates, environmental studies.
  3. Detailed design & procurement.
  4. Construction & installation.
  5. Commissioning, community training, operation & monitoring.
